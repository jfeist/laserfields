13 !!$ Compute the Faddeeva function w(z) = exp(-z^2) * erfc(-i*z),
14 !!$ to a desired relative accuracy relerr, for arbitrary complex
15 !!$ arguments z.
16 !!$
17 !!$ For sufficiently large |z|, we use a continued-fraction expansion
18 !!$ for w(z) similar to those described in:
19 !!$
20 !!$ Walter Gautschi, "Efficient computation of the complex error
21 !!$ function," SIAM J. Numer. Anal. 7(1), pp. 187-198 (1970)
22 !!$
23 !!$ G. P. M. Poppe and C. M. J. Wijers, "More efficient computation
24 !!$ of the complex error function," ACM Trans. Math. Soft. 16(1),
25 !!$ pp. 38-46 (1990).
26 !!$
27 !!$ Unlike those papers, however, we switch to a completely different
28 !!$ algorithm for smaller |z|:
29 !!$
30 !!$ Mofreh R. Zaghloul and Ahmed N. Ali, "Algorithm 916: Computing the
31 !!$ Faddeyeva and Voigt Functions," ACM Trans. Math. Soft. 38(2), 15
32 !!$ (2011).
33 !!$
34 !!$ (I initially used this algorithm for all z, but it turned out to be
35 !!$ significantly slower than the continued-fraction expansion for
36 !!$ larger |z|. On the other hand, it is competitive for smaller |z|,
37 !!$ and is significantly more accurate than the Poppe & Wijers code
38 !!$ in some regions, e.g. in the vicinity of z=1+1i.)
39 !!$
40 !!$ Note that this is an INDEPENDENT RE-IMPLEMENTATION of these algorithms,
41 !!$ based on the description in the papers ONLY. In particular, I did
42 !!$ not refer to the authors' Fortran or Matlab implementations, respectively,
43 !!$ (which are under restrictive ACM copyright terms and therefore unusable
44 !!$ in free/open-source software).
45 !!$
46 !!$ Steven G. Johnson, Massachusetts Institute of Technology
47 !!$ http://math.mit.edu/~stevenj
48 !!$ October 2012.
49 !!$
50 !!$ -- Note that Algorithm 916 assumes that the erfc(x) function,
51 !!$ or rather the scaled function erfcx(x) = exp(x**2)*erfc(x),
52 !!$ is supplied for REAL arguments x. I originally used an
53 !!$ erfcx routine derived from DERFC in SLATEC, but I have
54 !!$ since replaced it with a much faster routine written by
55 !!$ me which uses a combination of continued-fraction expansions

424  ! erfcx(x) = exp(x^2) erfc(x) function, for real x, written by
425  ! Steven G. Johnson, October 2012.
426  !
427  ! This function combines a few different ideas.
428  !
429  ! First, for x > 50, it uses a continued-fraction expansion (same as
430  ! for the Faddeeva function, but with algebraic simplifications for z=i*x).
431  !
432  ! Second, for 0 <= x <= 50, it uses Chebyshev polynomial approximations,
433  ! but with two twists:
434  !
435  ! a) It maps x to y = 4 / (4+x) in [0,1]. This simple transformation,
436  ! inspired by a similar transformation in the octave-forge/specfun
437  ! erfcx by Soren Hauberg, results in much faster Chebyshev convergence
438  ! than other simple transformations I have examined.
439  !
440  ! b) Instead of using a single Chebyshev polynomial for the entire
441  ! [0,1] y interval, we break the interval up into 100 equal
442  ! subintervals, with a switch/lookup table, and use much lower
443  ! degree Chebyshev polynomials in each subinterval. This greatly
444  ! improves performance in my tests.
445  !
446  ! For x < 0, we use the relationship erfcx(-x) = 2 exp(x^2) - erfc(x),
447  ! with the usual checks for overflow etcetera.
448  !
449  ! Performance-wise, it seems to be substantially faster than either
450  ! the SLATEC DERFC function [or an erfcx function derived therefrom]
451  ! or Cody's CALERF function (from netlib.org/specfun), while
452  ! retaining near machine precision in accuracy.
453
